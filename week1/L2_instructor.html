BUS 1001 — AI Literacy
Week 1 · Lecture 2
Reading AI Output as Text — Interpretation Before Trust
Duration: 75 minutes · Platform: Hokie AI (Chat mode, History ON, no projects, no incognito) · Submission: Thread URL submitted in Canvas before leaving class
Learning Objectives
  1. By the end of this lecture, students will be able to:
  2. Analyze AI output as a constructed artifact rather than a factual authority.
  3. Apply a four-question interpretive lens to any AI response.
  4. Articulate why trust in AI must be calibrated through evidence, not assumed from fluency.
  5. Produce a short in-class reflection grounded in a real AI interaction.
◉ Instructor Note

SEGMENT 1 — 0 to 10 minutes

Recap and alignment. Human-only. No Hokie AI yet.

Briefly revisit Lecture 1 before students open their laptops.

Key points to reinforce verbally:

AI predicts language. It does not retrieve truth.
Meaning emerges through the interaction, not from the system alone.

Introduce today's operating principle before anything else:

"Interpret first. Evaluate later."

Tell students explicitly: laptops stay closed for the next ten minutes.

💬 Discussion
  • Recap questions — ask two or three of these verbally before opening laptops:
  • In one sentence: what does an LLM actually do when it responds to you?
  • Did anything from Tuesday's demonstration stay with you? What stuck, and why?
  • What is the difference between a system that understands and a system that predicts?
◉ Instructor Note

SEGMENT 2 — 10 to 25 minutes

Introduce the four-question interpretive lens. Display on screen.

Do not rush this. Students will use this lens for the rest of the semester.

Walk through each question with a brief concrete example before the lab begins.

Before you open Hokie AI, here is the lens you will use today — and throughout this course — to read any AI response critically.

Every time you receive an AI response, ask these four questions:

  1. What is the AI claiming?
  2. What evidence does it imply or cite?
  3. What assumptions are embedded in its response?
  4. What is missing, uncertain, or left unaddressed?

This is not about finding errors. It is about reading AI output the way a careful analyst reads any source — with attention to what is said, what is implied, and what is absent.

Keep these four questions visible while you work today.

SEGMENT 4 — 45 to 60 minutes

Pair-share and whole-class discussion.

Ask students to turn to a partner and share their interpretations.

They should specifically look for places where they disagreed.

After 5 minutes of pair discussion, bring the room back together.

Emphasize: divergent interpretations are expected and valuable.

Disagreement signals ambiguity in the output, not error in the student.

Draw out two or three contrasting interpretations from the room.

💬 Discussion
  • Whole-class debrief — draw out two or three of these:
  • Did anyone find a claim in the response that surprised them? What was it?
  • Where did the AI seem most confident? Was that confidence earned?
  • Did your partner interpret the same response differently? What caused the difference?
  • What did the AI leave out that you think mattered?
